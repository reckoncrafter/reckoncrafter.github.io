[WRITING]

I feel like it’s fair to say that Galveston is a fairly normal city.

Many tourist destinations have their fair share of ghost stories, but Galveston is a bit notorious for them. Of course, why wouldn’t it be?

Its first buildings date back to the 18th century, and it literally has an Atlantis story.

In 1900, a hurricane so powerful that it broke the equipment used to measure it, made landfall on the then unprotected Galveston Island. It’s estimated that over 8,000 people died. To this day, it remains the deadliest natural disaster in U.S. history.

A once great city, with the economic might to rival New York, reduced to a floating pile of debris and corpses.

How could it not be haunted?

Now, I’ve lived here for a little over 5 years.

I’ve never really given much credence to the ghost stories I’ve been told about Galveston. After all, they’re all just things that tour guides say after you pay them $20 to walk through some old building. The ghost stories are for tourists, and for getting money out of tourists. I’ve almost never heard islanders just casually discussing hauntings.

However, I realize now that the stories do carry some truth.

There is something on this island that lingers, that remembers the great flood, that is still struggling to breathe trapped under the opaque, dirt filled water, it’s just not in the buildings.

Why would it be?

If a building survived the storm, it means that someone could have survived inside of it.

No, the buildings that stood after the storm had passed do not harbor whatever this is.

I generally don’t go walking on the beach at night.

Galveston’s not the safest city in the world, and the beaches aren’t really illuminated all that well.

Tonight though, I was particularly restless, and I figured that the salt air and the calming sound of waves washing against the shore would help relieve me of the anxiety plaguing my mind.

I saw it half an hour into my walk, just as I was going to turn back.

It was a coyote.

Now, there is a sizeable coyote population in the forested parts of the East End, and occasionally they’ll roam the easternmost streets late at night, but never have I seen one on the beach.

It was writhing, lying on its back, pawing frantically at the air as though it was trying to swim up through it. And the howl.. It began like a normal coyote’s howl, but its voice broke, and it turned into a moaning scream that glued my feet to the sand.

I could feel my heartbeat in every corner of my body. It was so strong in my neck that it felt like I was choking. I wanted to run, I wanted to cover my ears and get as far away as possible, as fast as possible, but I couldn’t take my eyes off of it.

It’s movements were almost human, the sounds it made sounded not like a coyote whimpering or crying in distress, but a person, screaming and shouting desperately for help, through the vocal structure of a canine.

And then, it looked at me. Right into my eyes. Immediately my mind was pierced with a level of fear and agony I’ve never experienced before. I felt the water around my nose and mouth, stopping my lungs. I felt the salt water seeping into the cuts and gashes made by the splintering mass of a collapsed house that had trapped me beneath.

I fell to my knees, desperately trying to breathe, shaking with pain and shock, and just as I began to regain some composure, I hear the coyote’s feet pat the sand as it trots away.

I sat there, on my hands and knees, trying to breathe slowly and stop the shaking, but I was making little progress. I decided that it’d be best if I just tried to get home first, then calm myself in the comfort of my abode.

So, I stumbled home, twitching with adrenaline and fear.

Needless to say, I didn’t even try to sleep that night. I stayed up until sunrise, nearly using up the last of my hash trying to calm down.

It worked well enough that I could finally take some deep breaths, but with the trauma, and the copious amount of cannabis in my system, it was clear that I was not going to be a functional member of society the next day.

A few months had come and went without any fanfare.

I had some recurring nightmares, but no real PTSD symptoms or anything, so me, being the tight upper lip bastard that I am, opted out of seeing a therapist. I just assumed that whatever therapist I told my story to, would assume I had a problem other than post-traumatic stress.

The details never blurred, but I was thinking about it less and less, mainly by avoiding the beach, and staying home at night.

I began to doubt if it had actually been real. I felt sure that it was, because I had a rather concrete timeline in my head, from me being sleepless, to going to take a walk on the beach, up to encountering the coyote, then trying to walk home shellshocked.

I didn’t entertain this questioning for much longer though, as I wasn’t terribly interested in scrutinizing every detail of a memory that still haunts my sleep.

Unfortunately, I wouldn’t get the opportunity to just let it fade.

It was another sleepless night, but this time, I recognized it as the same, specific feeling of restlessness I had the night I saw the coyote, and immediately I was filled with a sense of doom.

I decided to stay put. I definitely wasn’t going risk seeing something like that again.

I sat on my couch and sipped from a cup of tea.

The air got stuffier though, and it felt like it was getting hotter, too. I sighed, and got up to open up the window to let in the sea breeze, but as soon as I did, I heard it again.

The wailing, the broken howl.

Looking in the direction of the sound, I saw the coyote again, perhaps a different one, writhing and swinging desperately at an invisible surface that it was trapped under.

And again, it looked at me. This time though, I did not see the helpless, fearful eyes, pleading desperately for help, but now I felt burning hatred. Rage. I had failed to save it when it needed me, and now it hates me for not ending its suffering.

Yet, just as I was about to recover from the fear and panic, the furry beast trotted off again, east, back to its own home.

I’ve since put my house on the market.

My friends here are rather confused about why. I haven’t had any financial troubles, and my job is going well.

Every time they ask, I usually alternate between a few deflective platitutes:

“Just looking for a change of scenery”, “Seeking out greener pastures”, or “For business opportunities”.

I don’t feel like I can tell them the truth and have them believe me.

Let it be known though: Something still haunts Galveston Island. Something from all those years ago. Perhaps it’s just one lost soul fucking with the wild canine population, or perhaps it’s the collective anguish from some of those 8,000 dead. Those whose were swept out to sea, buried under the dirt and debris, or mangled as their bodies were dashed against the rocks by the unforgiving ocean.

The 1900 Storm was a tragedy, one that the people of Galveston know, but don’t remember. All, except for those for whom the storm never ended.
